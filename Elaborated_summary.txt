FAIRNESS IN FEDERATED LEARNING: A COMPREHENSIVE STUDY ON DYNAMIC CLIENT-ADAPTIVE FAIRNESS WEIGHTING

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This comprehensive project investigates the intersection of Federated Learning and machine learning fairness, specifically focusing on gender classification with attention to demographic disparities across racial groups. Conducted over four months under the direction of Prof. Manisha Padala, this study progresses from foundational Federated Learning concepts through the identification of inherent fairness issues in decentralized learning systems to the development and validation of a novel solution: Dynamic Client-Adaptive Fairness Weighting (DCA-FW). The research demonstrates that standard Federated Learning exhibits significant race-wise accuracy disparities, which are further amplified by adversarial attacks. The proposed DCA-FW method successfully reduces the fairness gap (from 0.0852 to 0.0507 on UTKFace and from 0.0992 to 0.0812 on FairFace) while maintaining stable overall accuracy, proving that client-level adaptive mechanisms are highly effective for addressing demographic bias in decentralized learning environments.

1. INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background and Motivation

Machine learning has become deeply integrated into critical decision-making systems across numerous domains, from healthcare diagnostics to credit scoring and criminal justice. However, these systems frequently exhibit systematic biases that disproportionately affect certain demographic groups, leading to unfair treatment and perpetuating societal inequalities. As machine learning models become more distributed and decentralized, particularly through Federated Learning paradigms, understanding and mitigating fairness issues becomes increasingly critical.

Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a privacy-preserving alternative to centralized machine learning, allowing multiple clients (devices, organizations, or individuals) to collaboratively train a global model without sharing their raw data. While FL addresses privacy concerns inherent in traditional centralized learning, it introduces new challenges for ensuring fairness. The decentralized nature of FL, combined with non-identical and non-independent (Non-IID) data distributions across clients, can amplify demographic biases and create disparities in model performance across demographic groups.

1.2 Project Objective

The primary objective of this research is to improve fairness in gender classification using Federated Learning while maintaining or improving overall model accuracy. Specifically, the project aims to:

1. Reduce race-wise accuracy disparity (measured as the fairness gap between the highest and lowest performing racial groups)
2. Maintain or improve overall model performance across all demographic groups
3. Develop a scalable and adaptive fairness method suitable for federated learning environments
4. Validate the proposed method across multiple datasets to demonstrate generalization

1.3 Project Scope and Timeline

This four-month research initiative follows a structured, iterative approach with weekly milestone reviews. The project is organized into four distinct phases:

Phase 1: Foundational FL Understanding using MNIST (Month 1)
Phase 2: Fairness Issues Identification on UTKFace (Month 1-2)
Phase 3: Fairness Improvement Methods Development (Month 2-3)
Phase 4: Validation on FairFace Dataset (Month 3-4)

2. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS AND BACKGROUND

2.1 Federated Learning: Core Concepts and Definitions

2.1.1 Federated Learning Architecture

Federated Learning is a decentralized machine learning paradigm where a global model is trained collaboratively across multiple independent clients without centralizing data. The fundamental architecture consists of:

Clients: Independent nodes (devices, organizations) that hold local data and perform local computation. Each client maintains its own dataset and trains a model locally.

Server (Central Aggregator): Coordinates the training process by aggregating model updates from clients and broadcasting the updated global model back to clients.

Communication Rounds: Iterative cycles where clients train locally and communicate updates to the server.

The Federated Averaging Algorithm (FedAvg) is the standard aggregation mechanism, mathematically defined as:

w^(t+1) = sum(n_k / n) * w_k^(t+1) for all clients k

where:
- w^(t+1) is the updated global model at round t+1
- n_k is the number of samples at client k
- n is the total number of samples across all clients
- w_k^(t+1) is the updated local model at client k after training for one round

Key advantages of Federated Learning include:
- Privacy preservation: Raw data never leaves the client
- Communication efficiency: Only model parameters are transmitted
- Scalability: Can accommodate thousands of clients
- Compliance: Meets regulatory requirements for data protection

2.1.2 Data Distribution Concepts: IID vs Non-IID

An essential concept in understanding Federated Learning behavior is the distribution of data across clients.

IID (Independent and Identically Distributed) Data:
IID data assumes that samples are randomly distributed across clients, such that each client has a representative sample of the overall data distribution. In IID settings, the distribution P(X,Y) is similar across all clients. This is the idealized scenario where clients have similar data distributions and model convergence is fastest.

Non-IID (Non-Independent and Non-Identically Distributed) Data:
Non-IID data represents the more realistic scenario where different clients have different data distributions due to natural geographical, demographic, or behavioral variations. In Non-IID settings:
- Different clients may have different class distributions
- Certain clients may have only a subset of classes
- Feature distributions may differ across clients
- Data is imbalanced differently on each client

Non-IID data significantly complicates Federated Learning:
- Slower convergence to the global model
- Potential accuracy degradation
- Increased vulnerability to attacks
- Exacerbation of demographic biases

2.1.3 Vulnerability to Adversarial Attacks in FL

Federated Learning systems are inherently vulnerable to poisoning attacks, where malicious clients introduce corrupted updates to degrade model performance. Label-flipping attacks represent one straightforward attack vector, where a malicious client deliberately flips labels from one class to another (e.g., flipping all "Class 1" labels to "Class 7" in a digit recognition task).

The impact of such attacks in Non-IID settings is particularly severe: if a malicious client is the only source of data for a particular class and it flips those labels, the global model may learn the incorrect mapping entirely, collapsing performance for that class to near zero.

2.2 Fairness in Machine Learning: Definitions and Metrics

2.2.1 Fairness: Core Concepts

Fairness in machine learning refers to the absence of unfair bias or discrimination against individuals or groups based on protected attributes such as race, gender, age, or other demographic characteristics. Machine learning model fairness can be viewed through multiple lenses:

Demographic Parity: The model makes similar predictions for different demographic groups, regardless of ground truth.

Equality of Opportunity (Equalized Odds): The model achieves similar true positive rates and false positive rates across different demographic groups.

Individual Fairness: Similar individuals (according to some criterion) receive similar treatment from the model.

2.2.2 Fairness Metrics Used in This Study

Statistical Parity Difference (SPD):
SPD measures whether a model's positive prediction rate is similar across demographic groups. Formally:

SPD = |P(Y_hat = 1 | Group_A) - P(Y_hat = 1 | Group_B)|

where Y_hat is the model's prediction. SPD ranges from 0 (perfectly fair) to 1 (maximally unfair). A value close to 0 indicates the model treats different demographic groups similarly in terms of positive predictions.

Equalized Odds Difference (EOD):
EOD measures the difference in true positive rates (also called equality of opportunity) and false positive rates across demographic groups. Formally:

EOD = |TPR_A - TPR_B| + |FPR_A - FPR_B|

where TPR (True Positive Rate) = TP / (TP + FN) represents the proportion of positive cases correctly identified, and FPR (False Positive Rate) = FP / (FP + TN) represents the proportion of negative cases incorrectly identified as positive.

A lower EOD value indicates fairer performance across demographic groups. EOD is particularly useful for measuring fairness in binary classification tasks.

Fairness Gap (Maximum Accuracy Disparity):
The fairness gap is defined as the difference between the highest and lowest group-wise accuracy:

Fairness Gap = max(Accuracy_race_i) - min(Accuracy_race_i) for all races i

This metric is intuitive and directly interpretable: it measures the maximum disparity in model performance across demographic groups. A fairness gap of 0.0852 means the highest-performing demographic group's accuracy is 8.52 percentage points higher than the lowest-performing group.

2.2.3 Sources of Bias in Machine Learning

Technical bias can arise from multiple sources:

Data Bias: The training dataset may not be representative of all demographic groups, or may contain imbalanced representation.

Algorithmic Bias: The model architecture or training procedure may inherently favor certain groups over others.

Feature Bias: The features used by the model may correlate with protected attributes in ways that introduce unfair discrimination.

Non-IID Data Distribution: In Federated Learning, different clients may have fundamentally different class and feature distributions due to geographical or demographic variation.

4. PHASE 1: FOUNDATIONAL FEDERATED LEARNING UNDERSTANDING (MNIST)

4.1 Objectives and Motivation

Phase 1 focused on establishing foundational understanding of Federated Learning mechanics, particularly:

1. Understanding how FL aggregation works compared to centralized learning
2. Observing the impact of Non-IID data distribution on convergence
3. Understanding FL vulnerability to poisoning attacks
4. Demonstrating how even a single malicious client can severely degrade model performance

4.2 Experimental Design

4.2.1 Experiment 1: Baseline FL with IID Data Distribution

Setup:
- Data randomly distributed across 5 clients (IID assumption)
- Standard FL training for 10 rounds
- No attacks or fairness interventions

Results:
- Global model achieved good convergence
- All clients contributed balanced gradient updates
- Convergence was relatively fast due to data homogeneity

Key Insight: Under IID conditions, FL behaves similarly to distributed SGD and converges relatively smoothly.

4.2.2 Experiment 2: Non-IID Data Distribution Impact

Setup:
- Data deliberately distributed non-uniformly across 5 clients
- Each client assigned only 2 out of 10 digit classes
- Standard FL training without attacks

Results:
- Convergence significantly slower
- Most clients converged to poor local optima due to limited class diversity
- Class 1 (data only on designated client): Accuracy improved from 0% to 98% on that client but was unstable globally
- Global Model Accuracy: ~73% (compared to typical >95% in centralized MNIST training)
- Non-IID distribution caused statistically significant accuracy degradation

Key Insight: Non-IID data distribution is problematic for FL convergence and accuracy. This challenge becomes even severe when combined with adversarial behavior.

4.2.3 Experiment 3: Label-Flipping Attack on Non-IID Data

Attack Setup:
- Single malicious client designated as the only source of Class 1 data
- Malicious client flips all Class 1 labels to Class 7
- Other 4 clients train normally on their classes
- 10 rounds of FL training

Attack Mechanism:
In each round, when the malicious client trains on Class 1 samples, it sees these samples as Class 7 (due to label flipping). It computes gradients that teach the global model to classify Class 1 features as Class 7. During aggregation, this corrupted gradient is combined with clean gradients from other clients. Since data is Non-IID, other clients don't have Class 1 data to counteract this corruption.

Results:
- Class 1 Accuracy: Dropped from 98% to 0%
- Global Model completely learned incorrect mapping for Class 1
- The global model's learned representation: Class 1 features → Class 7 output
- Accuracy on Class 7: Significantly degraded due to feature confusion

Key Insights and Implications:
1. Even a single malicious client can catastrophically corrupt learning for the classes it is responsible for
2. Non-IID data distribution amplifies the impact of poisoning attacks
3. FL lacks inherent defense against poisoning without explicit robustness mechanisms
4. In real-world scenarios with demographic minorities (analogous to small classes), such attacks could selectively harm underrepresented groups
5. This vulnerability motivates the need for fairness mechanisms in FL

4.3 Transition to Fairness-Focused Research

The Phase 1 experiments demonstrated two critical insights:

1. Non-IID data distribution is inevitable and problematic in real FL settings
2. FL systems are fragile to adversarial manipulation, particularly for underrepresented data portions

These observations motivated the hypothesis that Federated Learning systems might inherently exhibit fairness issues for demographic minority groups, even without explicit attacks. This hypothesis led to Phase 2, focusing specifically on fairness analysis in the UTKFace dataset.

- Subsequent Rounds: Accuracy fluctuates between 0.60-0.75

Attack Impact Asymmetry:
The attack disproportionately harms minority and weaker-baseline-performance racial groups:

Key Finding: Adversarial attacks disproportionately harm minority racial groups and those with weaker baseline performance. This suggests that FL fairness degradation under attack is not uniform but systematically favors already-favored demographic groups.

5.5 Analysis and Implications

5.5.1 Sources of Fairness Issues in Baseline FL

1. Data Imbalance: Different racial groups have different representation levels (White: 42.49%, Others: 7.14%), leading to different statistical populations and potentially different feature separability.

2. Feature Separability Variation: Different racial groups may exhibit different gender-specific facial features, causing the model to learn group-specific decision boundaries of varying quality.

3. Non-IID Client Data: With 10 clients and 5 racial categories, some clients may have higher concentrations of certain races, creating Non-IID conditions that amplify disparity.

4. Federated Learning Exposure: Minority racial groups receive less consistent exposure during aggregation (fewer gradient updates), potentially leading to weaker learning signals.

5.5.2 Why Attacks Amplify Fairness Issues

When malicious clients introduce corrupted gradients:
- These corrupted gradients affect all groups, but impact is not uniform
- Groups with weaker baseline features or smaller client-level presence suffer more severe degradation
- The FL aggregation process does not provide differential protection to vulnerable groups

5.6 Key Takeaway from Phase 2

Federated Learning baseline models on realistic data exhibit significant race-wise fairness disparities, even without adversarial intervention. These disparities are further amplified by attacks. This motivates the development of specific fairness interventions designed for FL environments.

6. PHASE 3: FAIRNESS IMPROVEMENT METHODS (DETAILED ANALYSES)

6.1 Overview of Fairness Intervention Strategies

Five distinct fairness improvement methods were developed, tested, and evaluated:

1. Static Weighted Cross-Entropy (Global)
2. Fairness Regularization
3. Adversarial Fairness Learning
4. Dynamic Client-Adaptive Fairness Weighting (DCA-FW)
5. Attack-Based Fairness Approach

Each method represents a different philosophical approach to addressing fairness, and each was carefully evaluated on the same baseline metrics for fair comparison.

6.2 Method 1: Static Weighted Cross-Entropy (Global Weighting)

6.2.1 Concept and Implementation

Motivation:
The intuition behind this method is straightforward: if certain demographic groups have lower baseline accuracy, increasing the weight of their contribution to the loss function should force the model to learn better representations for those groups.

Mathematical Formulation:
Let w_r denote the weight assigned to racial group r. The weighted cross-entropy loss for a batch is:

L_weighted = sum(w_r * CE_r) for all races r in batch

where CE_r is the cross-entropy loss for group r.

Weight Calculation:
Weights are computed once at the beginning using dataset statistics, based on inverse accuracy:

w_r = 1 / (baseline_accuracy_r + epsilon)

where epsilon is a small constant to prevent division by zero.

The underlying assumption: Groups with lower baseline accuracy receive higher weights, forcing the optimizer to allocate more gradient magnitude to learning these groups.

Key Characteristics:
- Static: Weights computed once, never updated
- Global: Same weights applied to all clients
- Dataset-based: Computed from data statistics, not actual model performance

6.2.2 Results

Fairness Gap: No significant improvement; sometimes worsens
Overall Accuracy: Reduced by 2-3%
Fairness Metrics (SPD/EOD): Minimal improvement

6.2.3 Analysis of Failure

Why Static Weighting Failed:

1. Weak Adaptation: The weights are static and based on dataset statistics, not the model's actual learning process. As the model converges, the relative importance of different groups changes.

2. No Real Signal: Assigning a higher weight to a lower-performing group at the loss level does not necessarily help the model learn better features; it only increases gradient magnitude, which can cause training instability.

3. Dilution in Federated Averaging: In FL, gradients from different clients are aggregated. Two phenomena occur:
   a) Clients with different data distributions assign different groupings to samples (a sample might be "White" on one client due to noise in data, but "Black" on another due to data distribution differences)
   b) During aggregation, the carefully calibrated weights from one client are averaged with weights from other clients, diluting the effect

4. Accuracy-Fairness Trade-off: The method forces the model to allocate more resources (gradients) to minority groups, which can degrade overall accuracy, particularly if the minority group's features are inherently harder to learn.

Conclusion: Static, dataset-based weighting is insufficient for fairness in FL. The method lacks adaptivity and is diluted during aggregation.

6.3 Method 2: Fairness Regularization

6.3.1 Concept and Implementation

Motivation:
Instead of weighting samples, this method adds a fairness-specific penalty term to the loss function, directly optimizing for the fairness objective.

Mathematical Formulation:
The overall training objective becomes:

L_total = L_CE + lambda * L_fairness

where:
- L_CE is the standard cross-entropy loss
- lambda is a regularization hyperparameter controlling fairness importance
- L_fairness is a fairness penalty term defined as:

L_fairness = max(loss_for_group_r) - min(loss_for_group_r) for all groups r

This penalty increases when there is a large disparity in group-wise losses, pushing the model toward more balanced learning.

Intuition:
By minimizing the difference between the highest and lowest group-wise losses, the model is encouraged to learn equally well for all demographic groups. When one group (e.g., Asian) has significantly higher loss than others (e.g., Indian), the regularization term increases, forcing the optimizer to focus more on the struggling group.

Key Characteristics:
- Global: Applied at the server or client level
- Adaptive: Loss values change dynamically as the model trains
- Loss-based: Operates on group-wise loss differences rather than class weights

6.3.2 Results

Fairness Gap: Worsened to 0.1013 (compared to baseline 0.0852)
Overall Accuracy: Dropped by 2-3%
Fairness Metrics (SPD/EOD): Minimal improvement

6.3.3 Analysis of Failure

Why Fairness Regularization Failed:

1. Loss vs. Accuracy Mismatch: The method minimizes loss disparity, not accuracy disparity. A group can have low loss due to high training accuracy on a limited subset of features, while overall accuracy remains poor due to overfitting or unstable learning.

2. Dilution in FL Convergence: During Federated Averaging, the regularization signal is diluted. Each client optimizes with its local fairness regularization, but when updates are averaged, the fairness objectives become conflicting. Client A might prioritize reducing loss for group X, while Client B prioritizes group Y. During aggregation, neither objective is fully achieved.

3. Sensitivity to Lambda: The hyperparameter lambda controls the strength of fairness regularization. Too low, and the fairness signal is weak. Too high, and the model sacrifices accuracy significantly. Finding the right balance is difficult and dataset-dependent.

4. No Group-Specific Learning: The method treats all groups uniformly, applying the same regularization penalty. It doesn't distinguish between groups that are "easy to learn" versus "hard to learn," potentially wasting regularization resources on already well-performing groups.

5. Conflict with Aggregation: In FL, each client has its own group-wise loss distribution. When server-side aggregation occurs, these local distributions are averaged out, and the global model's group-wise loss distribution may differ significantly from any individual client's distribution, causing conflicting gradient signals.

Conclusion: Fairness regularization is theoretically sound but fails in practice in FL settings due to aggregation dilution and loss-accuracy mismatch. The method needs to be adapted for federated settings where local objectives conflict with global objectives.

6.4 Method 3: Adversarial Fairness Learning

6.4.1 Concept and Implementation

Motivation:
The idea behind adversarial fairness is to learn representations that are task-relevant (good for gender classification) but invariant to protected attributes (race). If the model cannot extract race information from its learned features, it cannot exhibit race-based bias in predictions.

Mathematical Formulation:
Two networks are trained simultaneously in opposition:

1. Main Predictor Network P:
   Input: preprocessed image X
   Output: gender prediction Y_hat
   Objective: Minimize P(Y_hat = Y | X) - lambda * P_adversary(Z_hat)
   where the first term maximizes gender prediction accuracy and the second term minimizes the adversary's ability to predict race from x

2. Adversary Network A:
   Input: hidden layer features from P (denoted h(X))
   Output: race prediction Z_hat
   Objective: Maximize P(Z_hat = Z | h(X))
   Tries to predict race from the learned features

Training Procedure (Minimax Optimization):
In each training iteration:
1. Predictor P is updated to increase gender accuracy and decrease race predictability
2. Adversary A is updated to increase race prediction from learned features
3. These networks compete in a game-theoretic sense

Formal Objective:
min_P max_A [ L_CE(Y, P(X)) - lambda * L_Adversary(Z, A(h(X))) ]

Intuition:
If the adversary cannot predict race from the learned features, those features are race-invariant, and any bias in gender prediction must originate from true gender-predictive features, not demographic shortcuts.

Key Characteristics:
- Representation-level: Works at the feature learning level
- Theoretically grounded: Based on adversarial debiasing literature
- Task-aware: Focuses on making features invariant to race while preserving gender information

6.4.2 Results

Fairness Gap: Reduced to 0.0736 (improvement from baseline 0.0852)
Overall Accuracy: Decreased to ~85% (down from 87.3%)
Fairness Metrics (SPD/EOD): Moderate improvement

6.4.3 Analysis of Performance

Partial Success: Adversarial Learning Works:

1. Race-Invariant Features: The method successfully forces the model to learn features less correlated with race, reducing direct demographic bias in the learned representation.

2. Slight Accuracy Trade-off: The improvement comes at a small cost in overall accuracy, as the model must balance gender-relevant feature learning with race-feature separation.

3. Theoretical Soundness: The approach is well-grounded in fairness literature (Domain Adversarial Training, Adversarial Debiasing) and is known to work in centralized settings.

Limitations in FL Context:

1. Increased Complexity: Adding an adversary network increases model complexity and communication overhead in FL (larger models to transmit between clients and server).

2. Training Instability: Minimax optimization is notoriously unstable. In FL with Non-IID data, where different clients have different optimal adversary strengths, achieving stable convergence is difficult.

3. Incomplete Accuracy-Fairness Trade-off: While fairness gap improves to 0.0736, it remains higher than the later DCA-FW method (0.0507). The method achieves fairness but at the cost of accuracy, which is suboptimal.

4. Hyperparameter Sensitivity: The lambda parameter controlling adversary strength is sensitive. Too low, and the adversary's signal is weak. Too high, and the predictor collapses. Finding optimal lambda for distributed Non-IID data is challenging.

5. Potential for Cancel Representation: In extreme cases, the adversary-predictor competition can lead to features becoming uninformative for both tasks.

Conclusion: Adversarial Fairness Learning is theoretically sound and does improve fairness but introduces accuracy trade-offs and training complexity. It is a viable but not optimal approach for FL fairness.

6.5 Method 4: Dynamic Client-Adaptive Fairness Weighting (DCA-FW)

6.5.1 Concept and Mechanism

Motivation:
Unlike static weighting or global regularization, DCA-FW recognizes that Federated Learning is fundamentally a client-level process. The key insight is: what if clients themselves, particularly a subset of "fairness-aware" clients, dynamically compute and adjust their loss weights based on their own local model performance?

Core Idea:
At each federated round, designated "fairness clients" (approximately 30% of the client population) perform an additional computation step:

1. After local training, the fairness client evaluates its model on its local data, *computing local race-wise accuracy*.
2. The fairness client identifies which racial groups perform worse on its local data.
3. The fairness client adjusts its loss weights inversely to local performance: groups with lower local accuracy receive higher weights.
4. In the next training round, the fairness client uses these updated weights when training locally.
5. When the client sends its updated model to the server, these fair-weighted gradients are included in aggregation.

Mathematical Formulation:
Let a_r^k(t) denote the local race-wise accuracy for group r at client k in round t:

w_r^k(t+1) = 1 / (a_r^k(t) + epsilon)

where epsilon is a small constant.

In the next round, the weighted loss at client k becomes:

L_k(t+1) = sum(w_r^k(t+1) * CE_r^k(t+1)) for all races r

This ensures that each fairness client's gradient updates prioritize underperforming groups on that client.

Key Innovations:

1. Client-Level Adaptation: Weights are computed locally, not globally, preventing averaging and dilution.

2. Dynamic Updates: Weights change every round based on actual model performance, not static dataset statistics.

3. Per-Client Customization: Different clients may have different group-wise performance, so they use different weight sets, allowing heterogeneous fairness optimization.

4. Subset of Clients: Only ~30% of clients are designated as fairness clients, reducing computational overhead while still influencing the global fairness objective through FedAvg aggregation.

Intuition:
By having a subset of clients dynamically prioritize underperforming groups in their local training, the aggregated global model gradually learns to predict better for those groups. The clean gradients from these fairness clients blend with gradients from regular clients during aggregation, creating a global fairness effect without requiring all clients to be fairness-aware.

Fairness Gap: 0.0852 → 0.0507 (40% reduction)
Overall Accuracy: ~0.873 (maintained, no significant degradation)
Fairness Metrics (SPD/EOD): Significant improvement
Race-wise Accuracy:
- White: 0.879
- Black: 0.911
- Asian: 0.860
- Indian: 0.906
- Others: 0.869

Critical Achievement: DCA-FW achieves the best fairness improvement while maintaining overall accuracy, representing an optimal balance.

6.5.3 Analysis of Success

Why Dynamic Client-Adaptive Fairness Weighting Succeeds:

1. Aligned with FL Architecture: DCA-FW operates at the client level, which is the fundamental unit of FL. By making fairness decisions locally, it avoids the aggregation dilution problem that plagued Methods 1 and 2.

2. Performance-Based Adaptivity: Unlike static weighting, DCA-FW adapts based on the model's actual learned performance, not dataset statistics. As the model evolves, the weights evolve accordingly.

3. Each Client as an Agent: By allowing each fairness client to autonomously adjust weights based on its performance, the method harnesses the natural diversity of FL. Each client contributes not just a trained model but a fair-weighted model.

4. Reduced Overhead: Only 30% of clients need to perform the fairness computation (race-wise accuracy evaluation), keeping computational overhead manageable.

5. Direct Targeting: The method directly targets the fairness gap by increasing loss weights for underperforming groups, which directly correlates with improving their accuracy.

6. No Accuracy Trade-off: Unlike adversarial learning, DCA-FW does not introduce a fundamental accuracy-fairness trade-off. The method can simultaneously improve fairness and maintain accuracy because it works within the existing training objective (cross-entropy), merely reweighting its components.

7. Robust to Non-IID Data: The method benefits from Non-IID data because each client's local performance distribution is independent. A client with high Black accuracy and low Asian accuracy will prioritize Asian samples, contributing to global fairness naturally.

Theoretical Justification:
From a game-theoretic perspective, designating 30% of clients as fairness-aware clients creates a constraint equilibrium where the global model learns the gradients from both fair and regular clients. The fair clients' emphasis on underperforming groups is preserved and propagated to the global model through aggregation, while regular clients' gradients maintain overall accuracy.

6.5.4 Fairness Client Selection

In practice, fairness clients can be selected in multiple ways:

1. Random Selection: Arbitrarily choose 30% of clients to be fairness clients (used in this study).
2. Capacity-Based Selection: Designate clients with more computational resources as fairness clients.
3. Performance-Based Selection: Monitor clients' fairness performance over rounds and dynamically adjust participation.
4. Hybrid Approach: Combine multiple selection criteria.

In this study, random selection was used, and results were consistent across different random selections, suggesting the method is robust to client selection.

6.6 Method 5: Attack-Based Fairness Approach

6.6.1 Concept and Motivation

Motivation:
A hypothesis investigated was whether adversarial attack behavior could be leveraged to reduce fairness gaps. The reasoning: if we could strategically degrade the accuracy of already-high-performing demographic groups, the fairness gap (max - min accuracy) would shrink.

Approach:
Designate certain clients as "attack clients" that implement label-flipping or feature corruption specifically targeting the dominant demographic groups. The hypothesis: this would drag down the high performance of dominant groups, thereby reducing the fairness gap.

6.6.2 Results

Fairness Gap: Worsened to 0.1064 (compared to baseline 0.0852)
Overall Accuracy: ~0.884 (relatively maintained)
Behavior: Gap actually increased rather than decreased

6.6.3 Analysis of Failure

Why Attack-Based Fairness Failed:

1. Attacks Are Not Precision Instruments: Label flipping attacks introduce random noise that affects all groups, not selectively targeting dominant groups. The attack does not distinguish between groups.

2. Federated Averaging Dilution: In FL, only 30% of clients are attacking. During aggregation:
   Attack Gradient Updates from 30% of clients
   Clean Gradient Updates from 70% of clients
   
   Result: w^(t+1) = 0.3 * w_attack + 0.7 * w_clean
   
   The clean gradients from the majority dominate, diluting the attack effect significantly.

3. Attacks Hurt All Groups: When malicious clients introduce corrupted gradients, they don't selectively hurt the high-performing group; they corrupt the global model for all groups. The effect is a global accuracy loss distributed across all groups, not a strategic reduction in dominant group performance.

4. Counterintuitive Result: The fairness gap actually increased to 0.1064. Analysis revealed:
   - Attacks caused overall accuracy to drop slightly (from 0.873 to 0.884 is actually an anomaly due to test set variation)
   - The already-high-performing groups were more resilient to attacks due to strong feature learning
   - Low-performing groups were more vulnerable to attacks due to weaker initial learning
   - Result: Gap between high and low performers increased

5. Theoretical Mismatch: Fairness should be improved through better learning for underperforming groups, not through degrading others. Attempting to improve fairness through attacks contradicts the fundamental goal of improving model performance.

Conclusion: Attack-based fairness is fundamentally flawed. Fairness improvement must come from positive learning interventions, not destructive attacks. This approach exemplifies the principle that fairness is a constructive, not destructive, goal.

7. PHASE 4: DYNAMIC CLIENT-ADAPTIVE FAIRNESS WEIGHTING - DETAILED MECHANISM

7.1 DCA-FW Algorithm

Algorithm Overview:
```
For each federated round t:
  1. Server broadcasts global model w^t to all clients
  2. For each client k:
     a. Download global model w^t
     b. Perform local training on local dataset D_k for E epochs
     c. If k is a fairness client:
        i. Evaluate current local model on D_k
        ii. Compute local race-wise accuracies a_r^k
        iii. Compute weight adjustments w_r^k(t+1) = 1 / (a_r^k + epsilon)
        iv. Update loss function for next round: L_k ← weighted_L(w_r^k(t+1))
     d. Send updated model w_k^(t+1) to server
  3. Server aggregates: w^(t+1) = average(w_k^(t+1)) for all clients k
  4. Evaluate global model on global test set
  5. If t < T_max:
     - Go to round t+1
  Else:
     - Training complete
```

7.2 Fairness Client Selection

In this study, fairness clients were selected randomly (30% of total clients). This selection occurred once at the beginning of training.

Rationale for 30% Selection Rate:
- Balance between fairness influence and computational overhead
- Sufficient minority representation to influence aggregation (30% weight in FedAvg)
- Too low would have minimal global effect
- Too high would increase client-side computational burden

Alternative Selection Strategies (Not Implemented but Discussed):
1. Capacity-Based: Select clients with computational capability for fairness computations
2. Adaptive: Dynamically change fairness client membership based on performance
3. Responsibility-Based: Designate clients serving minority demographic groups as fairness clients
4. Voluntary: Allow clients to opt into fairness mechanisms based on organizational policies

7.3 Weight Computation Details

Local Race-Wise Accuracy Calculation:
For each fairness client k, at the end of each local training epoch set:

For each race r:
  accurate_predictions_r = sum(1 for sample s in D_k if s.race == r and model_predicts_correctly(s))
  total_samples_r = sum(1 for sample s in D_k if s.race == r)
  a_r^k = accurate_predictions_r / total_samples_r

Weight Update:
w_r^k(t+1) = 1 / (a_r^k(t) + epsilon)

where epsilon = 0.01 (prevents division by zero; ensures even well-performing groups get at least 1/1.01 ≈ 0.99 weight)

Weighted Cross-Entropy Loss:
For a batch B at round t+1:

L_fair = (1/|B|) * sum(w_r^k(t+1) * CE(y_i, y_hat_i)) for all samples i in B where sample i belongs to race r

Interpretation:
If a race has accuracy 0.9, its weight = 1 / 0.91 ≈ 1.10
If a race has accuracy 0.7, its weight = 1 / 0.71 ≈ 1.41

Therefore, underperforming groups receive proportionally higher weight in the loss, forcing the optimizer to allocate more gradient resources to learning better representations for these groups.

7.4 Global Model Aggregation

After each round, the server receives local models from all clients:

w^(t+1) = sum(n_k / n) * w_k^(t+1) for all clients k

where n_k is the number of samples in client k's local dataset and n is the total.

Key Point: The aggregation is weighted by data size, not uniform. Clients with more data contribute proportionally more to the global model. This is standard FedAvg and applies equally to fairness clients and regular clients.

7.5 Fairness Signal Propagation

How does fairness improvement propagate from 30% fairness clients to the global model?

Mechanism:
1. Fairness clients' updated models contain gradients weighted toward underperforming groups
2. During aggregation, these weighted gradients blend with clean gradients from regular clients
3. The aggregated global model w^(t+1) contains a weighted combination of fair and clean gradients
4. In the next round, all clients download this "fairness-influenced" global model
5. From this common starting point, fair clients continue adapting weights while regular clients train normally
6. Over multiple rounds, the fairness signal accumulates in the global model

Numerical Example (Simplified):
Suppose there are 10 clients, 3 are fairness clients:

Round 1:
- 3 fairness clients: Each optimizes with fairness weights, sends models w_fair
- 7 regular clients: Each trains normally, sends models w_regular
- Global model: w^(1) = (3/10) * avg(w_fair) + (7/10) * avg(w_regular)
- The fairness effect contributes 30% of the aggregated update

Round 2:
- All clients download w^(1), which already contains 30% fairness influence
- 3 fairness clients: Compute new local accuracy, update weights, train with new weights, send updated models
- 7 regular clients: Train normally
- Global model: w^(2) = (3/10) * avg(w_fair_updated) + (7/10) * avg(w_regular) + carries forward w^(1) fairness contribution
- Fairness effect accumulates over rounds

Over 10 rounds, fairness clients' contributions significantly influence the global model even though they represent only 30% of clients.

10. CONCLUSIONS

10.1 Key Findings

1. Federated Learning Inherently Exhibits Fairness Disparities
Even at baseline without adversarial intervention, standard FL models exhibit significant race-wise accuracy gaps (0.0852 on UTKFace, 0.0992 on FairFace). This suggests that FL, by virtue of its Non-IID distributed nature and client-level processing, naturally amplifies demographic biases present in data.

2. Traditional Fairness Methods Fail in FL Settings
Static weighting, global regularization, and adversarial learning, while individually sound in centralized learning, fail or underperform in FL due to:
- Aggregation dilution: Global signals are averaged away during FedAvg
- Conflict between local client objectives and global fairness objectives
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters in Non-IID settings
- Introduction of accuracy-versus-fairness trade-offs

3. Client-Level Adaptive Mechanisms are Essential
DCA-FW succeeds precisely because it operates at the level of individual clients, the fundamental unit of FL, rather than attempting to impose global constraints that are diluted during aggregation.

4. Dynamic, Performance-Based Adaptation Outperforms Static Methods
Unlike dataset-based static weighting, DCA-FW's performance-based dynamic weight updating allows the system to respond to the actual learning process, continuously adapting as the model converges.

5. Fairness Improvement Without Accuracy Sacrifice
DCA-FW achieves the unique property of improving fairness while maintaining or even slightly improving overall accuracy, representing an optimal solution rather than an accuracy-fairness trade-off.

6. Method Generalizes Across Datasets
Validation on FairFace confirms that DCA-FW is not an artifact of UTKFace's specific characteristics but represents a robust fairness mechanism applicable to different facial datasets, demographic distributions, and task settings.

10.2 Implications for Federated Learning Systems

10.2.1 Design Implications

1. Fairness Should Be a Design Consideration From Inception
Systems designed only for accuracy will inevitably exhibit demographic disparities. Fairness mechanisms like DCA-FW should be integrated into FL system architecture from the beginning.

2. Client-Level Instrumentation is Necessary
Fairness in FL requires that clients have the capability to compute demographic-level performance metrics. This requires:
- Metadata collection: Clients must label their data with demographic attributes
- Local evaluation: Clients must be able to compute group-wise metrics
- Dynamic parameters: Clients must be able to adjust training parameters based on performance

3. Transparency and Fairness Client Designation
The selection of which clients become "fairness clients" has ethical implications and should be transparent. Potential approaches include:
- Transparent random selection
- Client self-selection based on organizational fairness commitments
- Capacity-based selection ensuring clients have resources for fairness computations

10.2.2 Practical Implementation Considerations

1. Metadata Requirements
Clients must collect and track demographic attributes in their data. This requires privacy-preserving mechanisms:
- On-device demographic attribute prediction (if labels unavailable)
- Secure aggregation of demographic statistics
- Clear privacy policies addressing attribute data

2. Computational Overhead
DCA-FW fairness clients must compute group-wise accuracy, which requires:
- Local model evaluation: Computational cost is acceptable (same as standard validation)
- Data-to-group mapping: Overhead depends on demographic categories count
- Weight computation: Negligible (simple division operations)

3. Communication Efficiency
DCA-FW does not increase model size or communication overhead. Only model weights are transmitted, same as standard FL.

10.3 Limitations and Future Work

10.3.1 Limitations

1. Limited to Binary Task: This study focuses on binary gender classification. Multi-class fairness and multi-label scenarios require further investigation.

2. Race-Based Fairness: While race is studied, fairness across other attributes (gender identity, age, socioeconomic status) remains unexplored.

3. Fairness Client Ratio: In practice, determining optimal percentage of fairness clients (30%, 40%, 50%) requires more research. This may vary by application and demographic representation.

4. Communication Rounds: FL with 8-10 communication rounds is relatively small. Behavior in Settings with 100+ rounds requires investigation.

5. Attack Analysis Limited: While basic label-flipping attacks were tested, sophisticated poisoning attacks (Byzantine attacks) were not the focus and represent future work.

10.3.2 Future Research Directions

1. Extension to Multi-Class Tasks
Develop fairness mechanisms for multi-class classification where multiple protected attributes exist simultaneously (intersectionality).

2. Adaptive Fairness Client Selection
Rather than static selection, fairness client membership could be dynamically adjusted based on performance, allowing more effective allocation of fairness resources.

3. Alternative Weight Functions
Beyond 1/(a_r + epsilon), investigate other weight functions that may more effectively convert performance disparities into learning signals.

4. Theoretical Analysis
Provide theoretical convergence guarantees for DCA-FW, proving under what conditions the method converges to fair solutions.

5. Extension to Non-Classification Tasks
Explore fairness in FL for regression, ranking, and other task types beyond classification.

6. Centrality Analysis
Investigate how network topology affects fairness in FL with heterogeneous network conditions (some clients with high latency/bandwidth constraints).

11. KEY LEARNINGS AND TAKEAWAYS

As a comprehensive learning exercise on Federated Learning fairness, this project yielded the following key insights:

11.1 Federated Learning Fundamentals

1. The Decentralized Paradigm: FL handles data privacy by design but introduces new coordination complexities. The server never sees raw data, yet the aggregated global model somehow must represent the collective knowledge of all clients.

2. Non-IID Data is the Norm: In real-world FL, different clients have different data distributions due to geographical, organizational, or behavioral differences. This is not a special case but the expected scenario.

3. Aggregation is Critical: The aggregation function (FedAvg) is the linchpin of FL. It determines what signals are preserved and what signals are diluted. Any global-level fairness intervention must account for aggregation dilution.

11.2 Fairness in Machine Learning

1. Fairness is Multi-Dimensional: There is no single "fair" machine learning system. Fairness must be defined relative to a specific definition (demographic parity, equalized odds, etc.) and a specific protected attribute context.

2. Fairness-Accuracy Trade-offs are Not Inevitable: The belief that improving fairness necessarily degrades accuracy is not universal. With the right approach (like DCA-FW), fairness and accuracy can simultaneously improve.

3. Local Solutions Can Have Global Effects: By having clients locally optimize for fairness, the global model inherits fairness properties through aggregation, demonstrating that system-level objectives can emerge from local decisions.

11.3 Research Methodology

1. Comprehensive Baseline Understanding: Before developing novel methods, thorough understanding of the baseline problem is necessary. Phase 1 and Phase 2 established that fairness issues exist fundamentally in FL, motivating the research.

2. Iterative Development: The research proceeded through multiple fairness interventions (Methods 1-5), learning from failures why certain approaches don't work in FL settings.

3. Multi-Dataset Validation: Single-dataset testing is insufficient. Validation on multiple datasets (MNIST, UTKFace, FairFace) confirms generalization rather than dataset-specific artifacts.

4. Metrics Matter: Choosing appropriate metrics (SPD, EOD, fairness gap) enabled precise measurement of fairness, moving beyond subjective assessment.

11.4 Practical Wisdom

1. Simple Ideas Often Work Best: DCA-FW is conceptually simple (local weighted loss with performance-based weights) yet highly effective. Over-complicated methods (like adversarial learning) did not outperform the simple approach.

2. Failure Analysis is Instructive: Understanding why Methods 1-2 failed in FL (aggregation dilution) was as important as understanding why Method 4 succeeded. This led to the key insight that client-level operations are essential.

3. Fairness is not a Post-Hoc Patch: Attempting to add fairness to an existing unfair system (like attack-based Method 5) does not work. Fairness must be integrated into core training procedures.
